# The Activation Principle: How Value is Created in the Digital Age

## Summary

The debate over whether "data is the new oil" or "software is the new raw material" has served its purpose: it has forced us to look beyond the tangible. However, forcing digital reality into the conceptual mold of traditional raw materials creates more confusion than clarity. This article proposes the **Activation Principle** as a fundamental analytical tool: in the digital economy, value is not an inherent property of resources, but the result of their successful integration. No resource — physical, logical, or symbolic — generates value by itself; all require being "activated" through connection with other resources of a different nature. This perspective, grounded in the knowledge economy and systems theory, allows us to rethink production, consumption, and institutional design with greater precision and practical power.

---

## Introduction: The False Dilemma of Digital Raw Materials

For centuries, wealth was measured in tons of iron, barrels of oil, or bales of cotton. Raw materials were the unquestionable starting point: without them, production was impossible. Today, however, the most coveted resource is not extracted from mines, but generated with every click, every search, every digital interaction: **data**.

But data, by itself, is silence. A raw database tells no stories, predicts no behaviors, optimizes no processes. It needs to be questioned, cleaned, interpreted. Similarly, the most sophisticated hardware — a quantum computer, a state-of-the-art drone — is nothing more than a very expensive paperweight without the software to give it orders and purpose.

A computer is not the raw material for software, just as a book is not the raw material for the story it contains. The silicon and copper used to manufacture the processor **are** raw materials. The computer is a **finished product** that, paradoxically, needs to be "activated" to fulfill its function. This paradox is the gateway to a deeper understanding of how value creation actually works in our time.

We need, therefore, to abandon the attempt to stretch a 19th-century category to fit 21st-century reality. We need, instead, a **new explanatory principle**.

## Development: The Activation Principle

### 1. Definition of the Principle

The **Activation Principle** can be formulated as follows:

> In the digital economy, no resource — whether physical, logical, or symbolic — possesses intrinsic use value. Its effective utility emerges only when it is "activated" through integration with other resources of a different nature.

This principle has three immediate implications:

- **Value is relational, not substantial.** It is not in things themselves, but in the connections established between them.
- **The "death" of a resource is not its physical wear, but its isolation.** A computer without software is not broken; it is disconnected from the layer that gives it life.
- **Autonomous systems can activate each other.** A large part of value in the digital economy is generated through automated processes where direct human intervention is minimal or non-existent.

### 2. The Layers That Must Be Activated

For the principle to function as an analytical tool, we need to identify the major categories of resources that populate our digital ecosystem:

| Layer | Nature | Examples | What Activates It? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Physical** | Hardware, material infrastructure. | Computers, cables, sensors, antennas. | Needs software to control it and energy to power it. |
| **Logical** | Software, algorithms, protocols. | Operating systems, apps, social networks. | Needs hardware to run on and data to operate on. |
| **Symbolic** | Data, structured information. | Databases, records, digital images. | Needs algorithms to process it. |

None of these layers, in isolation, generates value. A computer without electricity or software is a very expensive paperweight. An AI algorithm without training data is a mathematical formula with no application. An ocean of data with no one to interrogate it is statistical noise.

### 3. The Role of Human Knowledge: Making Sense, Not Activating Everything

At this point, it is necessary to address a fundamental question: what role does human knowledge play in this scheme?

The temptation is to answer that the human being is the "universal activator," but this claim is problematic. In today's digital economy, enormous amounts of value are generated through automated processes where human intervention is minimal or non-existent in real time. A trading algorithm executes millions of transactions without any human "activating" each one. An AI system generates recommendations, translates texts, or recognizes faces without human knowledge intervening at the moment of activation. A smart thermostat "activates" the heating when the temperature drops, without anyone pressing a button.

Technological layers activate each other constantly, forming a fabric of automatic operations. Human knowledge **is not necessary for each specific activation**.

**So what is its function?**

Human knowledge fulfills an equally crucial but different function: **it is the interpreter that gives meaning to these activations.** Layers can connect and generate effects autonomously, but only human intervention — or design explicitly oriented toward human purposes — converts those effects into **meaningful value**.

A useful simile is that of language: words (logical layers) can combine following grammatical rules (algorithms) and generate correct sentences. But without a speaker who wants to say something, who interprets what is said, or who gives intention to the phrase, those sentences are just syntactic noise. Human knowledge does not generate each word, but it is what makes words *mean* something to someone.

The following table helps visualize this distinction:

| Activation Type | Does Human Knowledge Intervene? | Does It Generate Meaningful Value? | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pure Automated** | No (only in prior design) | Not necessarily | A server backs up at 3 AM |
| **Interpretive** | Yes, to analyze results | Yes, potentially | A scientist analyzes backup data |
| **Direct Operational** | Yes, in real time | Yes | A user opens an app and performs a task |
| **Unintended Emergent** | No | Unpredictable | Two autonomous systems interact in unforeseen ways |

Thus, the activation principle is nuanced: layers activate each other constantly, but the **direction**, **evaluation**, and **appropriation** of that fabric — that is, its conversion into human value — still depend on an observer, a designer, or a user who asks: what is this for? who benefits from this? does this make sense?

### 4. The Architecture of Activation

If value emerges from the interaction between layers, then the productive processes of the 21st century can be understood as **activation architectures**. Designing a product, a service, or a business model is essentially designing a system that makes these layers work together efficiently.

Consider an application like Uber:

- The **physical layer** consists of the drivers' and passengers' phones, and the vehicles.
- The **logical layer** is the application itself, its ride-matching algorithms and route calculations.
- The **symbolic layer** consists of real-time location data, ride history, and ratings.

Uber does not own vehicles (physical layer) nor directly generate location data (symbolic layer). Its genius lies in having designed an **activation architecture** that connects these layers efficiently, creating value that none of them could generate separately.

### 5. Practical Implications of the Principle

This framework allows us to rethink entire areas of our activity with greater precision.

#### Education: Forming Interpreters, Not Accumulators

If human knowledge is the giver of meaning, 21st-century education cannot be limited to transmitting information. It must form people capable of:

- **Understanding** the architecture of layers (digital literacy).
- **Interpreting** the results of autonomous systems (critical thinking).
- **Asking** relevant questions (because data without questions is mute).

A competent "interpreter" is not the one who accumulates the most data, but the one who knows what questions to ask and how to evaluate whether the answers make sense.

#### Innovation: Designing Architectures, Not Objects

Innovation ceases to be understood as the invention of a new object and becomes the design of a new **activation architecture**. Business opportunities no longer lie in owning a layer, but in creating the bridges between them.

- **Connectors:** Companies that facilitate integration (APIs, platforms).
- **Orchestrators:** Companies like Uber or Airbnb that design the complete system without owning most of the resources that compose it.

#### Sustainability: Extending Life Through Permanent Activation

From the Activation Principle perspective, obsolescence has two faces: **physical** (hardware breaks down) and **logical** (software stops receiving updates and becomes isolated from other layers). A functional computer without updated software is, for all practical purposes, a "dead" resource because it cannot be activated.

Therefore, a genuine sustainability policy must guarantee not only physical reparability, but the **right to keep devices active** through updatable software and legislation requiring manufacturers to guarantee long-term logical support.

#### Equity: Democratizing the Capacity for Interpretation

The true digital divide today is not access to hardware (which, though improvable, is increasingly widespread), but the **capacity for interpretation**. Two people with the same phone can experience radically different realities: one uses it only for passive consumption; the other, to learn, undertake, or access public services.

The digital equity of the future will not be measured by the number of devices per inhabitant, but by the distribution of the **competence to interpret** what those devices place within our reach.

---

## Conclusion: The Politics of Activation

The Activation Principle is not just an analytical tool; it is, above all, a **call to action**. If value is no longer in things, but in the ability to connect them intelligently, and if the meaning of those connections depends on our capacity to interpret them, then our institutions must be redesigned around this new reality.

- **Legislate for activation** means guaranteeing the right to connect (interoperability), the right to ask (access to public data), and the right to keep alive what we already have (logical support and the right to repair).
- **Educate for interpretation** means forming citizens who are not mere passive consumers of technology, but critical interpreters capable of evaluating whether the systems around us generate meaningful value.
- **Innovate for connection** means orienting talent and resources not toward the accumulation of objects, but toward the design of systems that multiply the value of what already exists.

The question we close with, therefore, is not whether data or software is the new raw material. It is a more practical, more political, and more human question: **Are we willing to reorganize our society around the idea that value is not possessed, but activated; and that the role of the human is not to activate everything, but to build what is missing and give meaning to what is already in motion?**
